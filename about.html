Onboarding an AI agent
like a new hire.

An AI agent is the fastest-learning teammate you'll ever hire — and it arrives with zero context about your business.

This app runs the first 90 days of an AI teammate on a credit-risk data-ops scrum team, modelled step-by-step on Michael Watkins' The First 90 Days. The question it answers isn't which model — it's whether a team knows how to onboard one: scoped work, a human reviewer, and trust earned in stages.

The premise

Not human versus AI. A new junior on the team.

Think back to your own first 90 days in a role. You were qualified — that's why you were hired. So why weren't you productive on day one? Somebody gave you context, small tasks, a reviewer, and slowly let go. day_9x does exactly that for an AI agent named Kai — and in the story, the humans are the mentors, reviewers and deciders. The agent is the junior.

The bet: the winners of enterprise AI won't be the ones with the best model — they'll be the teams that know how to onboard one.

Watkins, chapter 3 · the diagnostic

STARS — read the situation before you onboard

You don't onboard someone into a burning crisis the same way you onboard them into a calm, healthy team. STARS is Watkins' name for five situations a leader can inherit — Start-up, Turnaround, Accelerating growth, Realignment, Sustaining success. In day_9x, the situation you pick (on the Charter tab) changes the pace of onboarding: how long the agent quietly shadows before it's trusted with scoped work, and how fast it earns full autonomy.

◆ In the boardroom

STARS makes the onboarding plan honest about context. A crisis backlog (Turnaround) gets a short watch-and-learn and fast, tight-leashed wins. A brand-new capability (Start-up) invests up front in learning because there are no runbooks yet. The same tool, tuned to the situation you're actually in.

◇ In the engine room

Each STARS key carries two fractions — shadow_frac and gated_frac — that split the timeline into Shadow → Gated → Autonomous bands. Choosing a situation on the Charter tab re-derives the sprint plan and the phase widths; everything downstream (checkpoints, breakeven pacing) follows from it.

The approach · how the agent is loaded onto the team

Five stages, the same shape as onboarding a person

The tabs above are the agent's 90-day journey, in order. Nothing is skipped; trust is only ever spent after it's earned.

1
DAY 0

The Charter

Watkins' Five Conversations, written down

Before any work, the team agrees what the agent is for: which tasks are in scope, who reviews it, and — explicitly — the “stays human” list of decisions it will never make.

Under the hood: the STARS situation + a duration slider (default 90 days) derive a sprint plan; the five conversations (situation, expectations, resources, style, development) become the agent's operating contract.

2
DAYS 1–N

Shadow mode

Read-only · cited · knows when to say “I don't know”

The agent answers questions from the team's runbooks with citations, and abstains when it has no grounding — raising its hand instead of guessing. Silent, confident wrongness is the failure mode being designed out.

Under the hood: retrieval-augmented generation over the team corpus with a similarity floor; below it, the agent refuses and logs a knowledge gap for a human to teach later.

3
DAYS N–M

Early wins

Scoped tasks behind an approval gate

Small, verifiable tasks (triage an incident, draft a data-quality rule). A human buddy approves, rejects, or receives an escalation on every one. Approvals are the evidence that builds trust.

Under the hood: a deterministic drafting backbone per task class, an escalation path for anything out-of-glossary, and a per-class ledger that records every human decision.

4
DAY 90

Probation review

Autonomy decided by evals, not vibes

Each task class must pass its own quality bar before the agent is trusted to act on its own — exactly like a person's 90-day review. Fail, and it stays supervised.

Under the hood: a golden-set eval harness gates promotion by exit code; two “culture” cases stay red until a human coaches the unwritten rule in — then all gates go green.

5
ONGOING

Breakeven & retro

The number a COO remembers

A new hire consumes value before creating it. day_9x shows the breakeven curve in analyst-hours — and which hours each named human got back, shifting from doing to reviewing.

Under the hood: a value ledger (hours consumed in review vs. created by verified output) plots the crossover; STARS changes when it happens — a turnaround breaks even in ~2 sprints, a start-up nearer ~6 weeks.

The mechanism · trust as a threshold

Autonomy is earned per task class — and it's revocable

Trust isn't a personality trait the agent has or lacks; it's a state it holds per kind of work, backed by evidence. It can be granted, held, or taken back.

SHADOW
Drafts only

Everything is reviewed. Zero autonomy. The agent watches and answers, but acts on nothing.

GATED
Acts, with sign-off

Unlocked when the task class passes its eval gate. The agent may act — but a human approves every output.

AUTONOMOUS
Acts, lightly audited

Offered only when the gate holds and enough gated work was verified. Even then, a sample is still audited.

SHADOW → GATED → AUTONOMOUS  ·  per task class  ·  evidence, not seniority

The differentiator · v2.0

The knowledge miner — where the agent teaches the team back

Most of what makes a team effective is never written down — it lives in people's heads. When the agent hits one of those gaps, it doesn't just fail quietly. It interviews the expert, and turns the answer into reusable, credited team knowledge.

Agent abstains→ Gap logged→ Agent interviews the SME→ Versioned, attributed Skill→ Now answers, with a citation

◆ In the boardroom

This is the answer to the fear. The expert isn't replaced — they're credited. A “Teachers of the sprint” board shows whose knowledge is now answering the team's questions. Attribution is what turns the instinct to hoard know-how into the incentive to teach it.

◇ In the engine room

The gap register captures every abstain as a structured lead; the agent drafts interview questions; the SME's answer is written to a versioned Skill file (coached by · date · source gap), re-indexed, and immediately citable. Freshness and access-control rules keep the corpus honest.

Why a stakeholder cares

Everything reduces to hours, trust, and governance

sprints, not months
Watkins puts a manager's breakeven near six months. For an AI teammate both sides are measurable, so the crossover lands in a handful of sprints — and you can see the day it happens.
two clocks
15-day sprints pace the work; a report every 9 days paces stakeholder communication. A sponsor never waits a full sprint for a signal — that's the “9x” in the name.
$0 · keyless
This whole demo runs with no API key and no cost — safe to share publicly. Governance held on purpose: no secrets on the public app, all data synthetic.
